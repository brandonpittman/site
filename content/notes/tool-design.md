# Tools Make Promises — Outline

## 1. The Promise

Every tool makes a promise by its design. Victorinox names telegraph it — Farmer, Harvester, Rancher, Climber. When the name is accurate, the promise is legible. Coverage vs. fidelity is the fundamental design axis.

## 2. EDC vs. EDU

Carrying every day is not the same as using every day. Most gear discourse conflates them. EDC is availability against unknown future problems. EDU is task-fitness for known, frequent ones. Getting this wrong sets up false expectations.

## 3. Expectations Have to Match the Promise

The tool didn't fail — your expectations were wrong. Phenomenological point: readiness-to-hand depends on understanding what it's ready _for_. The Minichamp isn't low-fidelity by accident — low fidelity is the design.

## 4. Context Changes What You Can Afford

Out of the house: wide coverage, low fidelity acceptable, carrying constraints are real. In the house: wide coverage, high fidelity available, no carrying constraints. Same logic, different expression. The framework doesn't change — the context does.

## 5. Design Trade-offs Aren't Yours to Make

Victorinox is capturing markets you're adjacent to, not designing for you specifically. You get the toolset you wanted packaged in ways you didn't ask for. Design grievances are real but secondary to coverage and fidelity. The question is always: is what I'm getting worth what I'm accepting?

## 6. The Framework Comes From the Problem

Didn't start with coverage and fidelity — started with needing scissors for zip ties. Patched gaps without seeing the whole picture. Framework emerged from iterating repeatedly on the same problem. Once you have it, you can evaluate the whole picture at once instead of chasing individual gaps.
